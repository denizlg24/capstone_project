\subsection{Context}

This report documents the work carried out in the Capstone Project course unit, part of the Bachelor's in Informatics and Computing Engineering at the Faculty of Engineering of the University of Porto (FEUP), during the second semester of the 2025/2026 academic year.

The project was developed at \textbf{INESC TEC}, under the supervision of Professor Nuno Paulino and the tutorship of Professor João Cardoso. INESC TEC is an associate laboratory active in engineering and computer science. The work was integrated in the \textit{Optical and Electronic Technologies} area of the \gls{ctm} centre (Centre for Telecommunications and Multimedia), which hosts applied research in compilation and reconfigurable arrays, the areas in which this project is positioned.

The work is integrated in \textbf{\gls{aimacov}}, an ongoing national research project of the host team. Within that effort I was integrated in a working group of five colleagues, accompanied by the project's grant holders, who provided technical guidance throughout the semester.

The goal was to investigate and implement an approach inspired by \gls{lisa}~\cite{lisa2022}, based on \textit{Graph Neural Networks} (\glspl{gnn}), for \textit{\gls{mapping}} of \textit{Data Flow Graphs} (\glspl{dfg}) onto \textit{Coarse-Grained Reconfigurable Arrays} (\glspl{cgra}).

\subsection{Technical concepts}

Before going further, this subsection gives a quick overview of the main ideas behind the project, so the rest of the report can be read without jumping to external references.

\paragraph{CGRA.}
A \textit{Coarse-Grained Reconfigurable Array} is a hardware accelerator made of a grid of small \textit{Processing Elements} (PEs), usually arranged as a 2D mesh. Each PE is basically an ALU with a tiny register file, and PEs talk to their direct neighbours through a fixed interconnect. The difference from an FPGA is the granularity: FPGAs are reconfigured at the bit level, while CGRAs are reconfigured at the word level. Each cycle, every PE is told which operation to run and where to read its operands from. This makes CGRAs a good fit for compute-heavy kernels with regular data flow, because they give up some of the flexibility of an FPGA in exchange for better energy efficiency and much smaller configuration data.

\paragraph{Mapping process.}
\textit{Mapping} is the compilation step that decides which PE runs each operation and, if cycles are also considered, when. The input is normally a \textit{Data Flow Graph} (DFG): a directed acyclic graph where nodes are operations and edges are data dependencies. The mapper has to respect three kinds of constraints: \emph{operation compatibility} (a node can only go on a PE that supports its opcode), \emph{connectivity} (if $u \to v$ is a dependency, the PEs assigned to $u$ and $v$ must be directly connected, or the value has to be routed through other PEs or memory), and \emph{resource limits} (no PE runs more than one operation in the same cycle, fan-in/fan-out budgets are respected, and so on). Mapping is NP-hard in general, so the literature has explored both exact approaches (SAT/ILP-based) and heuristics.

\paragraph{Graph Neural Networks.}
A \textit{Graph Neural Network} (GNN) is a type of neural network that works directly on graphs. Each node starts with a feature vector and, through several rounds of message passing, mixes in information from its neighbours. After a few rounds, each node ends up with an embedding that captures both its own features and the structure around it, which can then be used to predict per-node, per-edge or per-graph properties. GNNs fit DFG-based problems naturally, because the input is already a graph and the things one usually wants to predict (scheduling priorities, distances, placement hints) live on the nodes.

\paragraph{LISA.}
\gls{lisa}~\cite{lisa2022} is a GNN-based approach for mapping DFGs onto spatial accelerators. Instead of trying to solve the mapping itself, the GNN is trained on traces from a reference mapper to predict a few labels per DFG node, such as scheduling order and spatial/temporal distances to dependent nodes. These labels are then handed to a downstream mapper as constraints, which nudges it towards solutions similar to the reference flow while staying portable across different accelerator targets.

\paragraph{SAT-MapIt.}
\gls{satmapit}~\cite{satmapit2023} is an exact, SAT-based mapper for CGRAs. It encodes the mapping problem as Boolean constraints over placement variables, and uses a \textit{Kernel Mobility Schedule} (\gls{kms}), a modulo-scheduling construct that bounds the cycles in which each operation can be issued, to shrink the search space before the SAT solver kicks in. The output is either a correct mapping (placement plus modulo schedule) or a proof that no mapping exists for the given resource budget.

In this project, LISA and SAT-MapIt are the two main references that the prototype combines: a LISA-style GNN provides per-node priorities, and a simplified, SAT-MapIt-inspired mapper checks each one-shot partition built from those priorities.

\paragraph{Related approaches.}

At the other extreme, heuristic mappers such as DRESC \cite{mei2003dresc} use simulated annealing to solve placement, scheduling and routing simultaneously, trading optimality guarantees for tractable compile time on larger graphs. SAT-based approaches such as Miyasaka et al. \cite{miyasaka2020sat} encode the mapping constraints directly as a Boolean formula, while SAT-MapIt \cite{tirelli2023sat} narrows the search space by computing a Kernel Mobility Schedule and then applying SAT to find an optimal modulo schedule, but remain targeted at the single-configuration placement problem rather than multi-partition spatial mapping.

Traditional approaches apply generic graph-partitioning algorithms such as the Kernighan-Lin heuristic or multi-level frameworks such as METIS \cite{karypis1998metis} to minimize edge cuts. These methods are agnostic to CGRA topology and routing constraints, so the partitions they produce are not guaranteed to be embeddable onto the target hardware. Greedy heuristics reduce computational cost further but frequently fail on wide, parallel graphs and produce high cut counts when they do succeed, each cut edge introducing communication overhead in the form of a synthetic memory operation. Closer to our setting, Kojima et al. \cite{kojima2022partitioning} apply deep learning to make kernel partitioning mapping-aware, steering the partitioner toward sub-DFGs that are more likely to map successfully. Unlike our approach, the learned model assesses partition mappability rather than directly guiding topology-feasible cuts with SAT-derived supervision.

\subsection{Goals and expected results}

The goals defined for the project were the following:

\begin{itemize}
	\item Study the state of the art on \textit{mapping} of DFGs to CGRAs, in particular approaches that leverage AI to improve mapping times.
	\item Build a mapping flow that uses AI to produce high-quality mappings in a fraction of the time of a purely algorithmic approach.
	\item Evaluate the proposed flow and contrast it with other purely algorithmic strategies.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Methodology}

This project was conducted at INESC TEC in a hybrid model. I had the
flexibility to work on the project remotely and to communicate with my
supervisors and the rest of the team through a Slack\cite{slack} workspace
that belongs to the team and to which I was invited.

Throughout the process, weekly meetings were held in the INESC TEC room
in which I presented what had been accomplished during the week and we
discussed the results and what should be addressed in the following one.
For these meetings I prepared a short presentation of about 5-10 minutes.
These meetings were an opportunity not only to clarify questions about the
project and its implementation, but also to receive feedback from the supervisors
regarding the current state of the prototype and the features to be
integrated next. This is also why the project requirements evolved
significantly over the semester: although the main objective was clear
(use \textit{AI} to guide the mapping process), the
structure of the pipeline and exactly which state of the art approaches to follow
were not fixed at the start, and the meetings were essential to decide
which ideas were appropriate given the project's goals and duration.

The development overall followed an iterative
methodology\cite{despa2014comparative}, well suited to a research project
of this nature. The project was developed inside a private
GitLab\cite{gitlab} repository belonging to the team.

The technical resources used were:

\begin{itemize}
	\item GitLab\cite{gitlab} for version control.
	\item Slack\cite{slack} for day-to-day communication with the team.

\subsection{Activities carried out}

The technical activities can be grouped into the following blocks:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Study}: literature review on the state of the art on mapping DFGs to CGRAs, in particular approaches that leverage AI to improve mapping times.
	\item \textbf{Development}: development of the proposed GNN-Guided Graph Partitioning for Spatial CGRA Mapping framework.
	\item \textbf{Evaluation}: execution of a benchmark which compares the proposed approach with purely algorithmic methods, and evaluation of the results.
	\item \textbf{Paper writing}: writing of a paper that describes the mapping approach which is submitted for publication in INForum 2026~\cite{inforum2026}.
\end{itemize}

The initial phases (study, prototyping with state-of-the-art tools) took place between the start of the semester and mid-March;
the GNN-Guided Graph Partitioning for Spatial CGRA Mapping framework and benchmarks were consolidated in late May;
the paper and report writing followed in the final weeks.

The remainder of this section walks through the project's evolution
meeting by meeting, which is the most faithful way to describe how the
direction of the work was iteratively reshaped.

\subsubsection{First meeting (20 February 2026)}

The first meeting was used to re-introduce the team, agree on the
communication channel (Slack) and align on the immediate reading
material. I was tasked with surveying the state of the art on graph
\textit{mapping} for CGRAs, in particular GNN-based approaches, so that
the next meeting could be grounded on concrete papers rather than on the
generic proposal.

\subsubsection{Second meeting (27 February 2026)}

After the initial reading pass, the supervisors asked me to bring a
dataset of DFGs of the kind used in the state-of-the-art papers, to
review two or three of the most cited mappers, and to try out a
conventional mapper as a baseline. The discussion also opened two
questions that would stay with the project until the end: how to check
that a \textit{mapping} is valid, and how to represent a CGRA as a
graph. The recommendation was to keep both representations as simple as
possible.

\subsubsection{Third meeting (6 March 2026)}

By the third meeting I had cloned the LISA~\cite{lisa2022} DFG generator
and produced a first synthetic dataset of graphs. Reproducing the LISA
flow end-to-end ran into a practical blocker: LISA itself relies on
\textit{Gurobi} on top of an \textit{LLVM} build, and the \textit{LLVM} dependency could not be
installed cleanly in the working environment. In parallel, I started
reading SAT-MapIt~\cite{satmapit2023}, which uses a \textit{Kernel Mobility
	Schedule} (KMS), a construct from \textit{modulo scheduling} that bounds the
cycles in which each operation may be issued, to reduce the SAT search
space. The key idea that came out of this meeting was the one that ended up
defining the whole project: rather than picking one of the two
approaches, combine them. Use the LISA-style GNN to add constraints to
the graph, and let a SAT-based mapper consume those constraints.

\subsubsection{Fourth meeting (20 March 2026)}

Two things were settled in this meeting. First, the LISA flow was
reproduced for the homogeneous case: 1000 DFGs were generated for a
$4 \times 4$ CGRA and another 1000 for an $8 \times 8$ CGRA, although
mapping all of them with the reference flow turned out to be impractical
(roughly 40 graphs mapped in a full day of runtime for the $4 \times 4$
case). Second, I started reading \textit{NaviMap}, a GNN-plus-\textit{deep-Q-network}
mapping approach, and we discussed whether the LISA labels could be fed
into a NaviMap-like agent. The idea was left open as a possible third
leg of the prototype, to be evaluated in the following meeting after a
deeper read of the paper.

\subsubsection{Fifth meeting (27 March 2026)}

This meeting was when the NaviMap direction was dropped. After a closer
read, combining a \textit{deep-Q-network} agent on top of the GNN labels turned
out to add significant implementation and training complexity, having to
handle different input shapes for the neural networks as well as a lack of
open-source availability from NaviMap, without a clear advantage
over the simpler LISA-plus-SAT-MapIt pairing for the scale of graphs we
were targeting. The decision was therefore to set NaviMap aside and to
concentrate the remaining effort on the LISA-style labels combined with
the SAT mapper.

\subsubsection{Sixth meeting (10 April 2026)}

The discussion in this meeting was almost entirely about the
\textit{codegen} side of the project, led by Francisco. From my side,
the main takeaway was that the mapping flow had to produce a clean,
stable JSON output, so that the downstream code generation could
consume it without further negotiation about the format.

\subsubsection{Seventh meeting (17 April 2026)}

At this point I had a Python \texttt{CGRA} class that is passed to the
mapper, LISA was producing four labels per node (used as features), and
the experimental SAT path was using two of those labels (temporal and
spatial distance) on top of a \textit{CGRA-ME}-style template to produce
reference mappings for that prototype stage. The meeting surfaced a real, recurring problem: the DFG
formats used by LISA and by SAT-MapIt did not agree. LISA worked with a
small set of operations (\texttt{const}, \texttt{load}, \texttt{store},
\texttt{add}, \texttt{mul}, \texttt{sub}, \ldots), whereas SAT-MapIt
also reasoned about branches, \textit{live-ins}/\textit{live-outs} and other entities.
The action item was to consolidate a single input format and intermediate
JSON, under the assumption that everything is one-shot, and to check
whether SAT-MapIt could be coerced into a one-shot regime. This meeting was
when we decided to simplify the problem by focusing on one-shot mapping with no temporal constraints.

\subsubsection{Eighth meeting (24 April 2026)}

This is the meeting in which the eventual shape of the prototype was
decided. The problem on the table was that SAT-MapIt is built around
\textit{modulo scheduling}, its notion of an \textit{initiation
	interval}, and the way it places operations across cycles all assume
that regime, whereas LISA does not directly emit a SAT-MapIt KMS or a
complete modulo schedule. It predicts labels such as schedule order and
spatial/temporal distances that guide a downstream mapper. Plugging the LISA
labels straight into SAT-MapIt was therefore not straightforward. Due to time constraints and the scope of the Capstone Project,
the team decided to simplify and abstract the mapping into one-shot mapping. This would raise
an issue, where the LISA labels would be pointless since all the nodes would be placed at timestep
0. My suggestion was to invert the approach: take a large DFG, run a learned model on it to obtain
per-node features, use those features to \emph{partition} the graph in such
a way that cut dependencies are made explicit through synthetic memory
operations, and then one-shot
\textit{map} each subgraph with the SAT mapper. The open question
became how to perform the partition itself, whether by a heuristic or by
another learned component.

\subsubsection{Ninth meeting (30 April 2026)}

The ninth meeting confirmed that the partitioned one-shot strategy
worked. A greedy partitioner was already in place, not yet driven by a
GNN, but by hand-crafted priorities such as \textit{ASAP}, \textit{ALAP} and edge count —
whose objective was to minimise the number of edges cut at the
partition boundary, and thus the amount of synthetic memory traffic
needed to keep memory operations outside of the CGRA's ALU array. The
splitting was working and each subgraph was being mapped one-shot with
the SAT-MapIt-inspired one-shot mapper to produce reference labels for
this abstraction. From here the plan was
clean: use the original large graph, augmented with the per-partition
timesteps as an extra feature, to train a GNN that predicts the
schedule order (\textit{label0}); then feed \textit{label0} into the
partitioner to obtain better partitions, and finally validate each
partition with the simplified SAT mapper.

\subsubsection{Tenth meeting (14 May 2026)}
At the tenth meeting, I presented the work done on the GNN-guided partitioner. A key detail was to generate better partitions for training data. An exhaustive algorithm was developed that iterates through all possible partitions in an upward sweep and, for each partition, checks if it is mappable with the SAT mapper. If it is, the partition is stored as a candidate, and the algorithm continues to the next partition. The algorithm continues until all possible partitions have been checked, and at the end, the best partition (the one with the lowest amount of cut edges) is selected from the candidates. This exhaustive approach ensures that the best possible partition is found for each DFG, which can then be used to train the GNN-guided partitioner.
Also, I brought up a detail that was discovered while running the first benchmark on the greedy GNN-guided partitioner. Although the GNN predictions would improve the partitioning, a large amount of DFGs were still unmappable. The reason was that the greedy partitioner was not aware of the CGRA's topology and routing constraints, so it could produce partitions that were not embeddable onto the target hardware. The solution proposed in this meeting was to implement a new kind of topology-aware algorithmic partitioner that would be aware of the graph and CGRA topologies and would respect their routing constraints. This entailed a new step in the partitioning process, the repair step, where a partition would be checked with the SAT mapper and if it was not mappable, the partition would be repaired by moving nodes to other partitions until it was mappable. Combining this topology-aware partitioner with the GNN-guided predictions would allow it to produce partitions that were mappable and would reduce the number of cut edges, which was the main goal of the partitioning process.

The final meetings were dedicated to preparing and reviewing the
scientific paper that resulted from the project, and are grouped here because
they formed a continuous writing and revision process.
