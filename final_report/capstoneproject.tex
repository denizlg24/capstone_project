\subsection{Context}

This report documents the work carried out in the Capstone Project course unit, part of the Bachelor's in Informatics and Computing Engineering at the Faculty of Engineering of the University of Porto (FEUP), during the second semester of the 2025/2026 academic year.

The project was developed at \textbf{INESC TEC}, under the supervision of Professor Nuno Paulino and the tutorship of Professor João Cardoso. INESC TEC is an associate laboratory active in engineering and computer science. The work was integrated in the \textit{Optical and Electronic Technologies} area of the \textbf{CTM} centre (Centre for Telecommunications and Multimedia), which hosts applied research in compilation and reconfigurable architectures, the areas in which this project is positioned.

The work is integrated in \textbf{AIMACOV}, an ongoing national research project of the host team. Within that effort I was inserted in a working group of five colleagues, accompanied on by the project's grant holders, who provided technical guidance throughout the semester.

The goal was to investigate and implement an approach inspired by LISA\cite{lisa2022}, based on \textit{Graph Neural Networks} (GNNs), for the \textit{mapping} of \textit{Data Flow Graphs} (DFGs) onto \textit{Coarse-Grained Reconfigurable Architectures} (CGRAs).

\subsection{Technical concepts}

Before going further, this subsection gives a quick overview of the main ideas behind the project, so the rest of the report can be read without jumping to external references.

\paragraph{CGRA.}
A \textit{Coarse-Grained Reconfigurable Architecture} is a hardware accelerator made of a grid of small \textit{Processing Elements} (PEs), usually arranged as a 2D mesh. Each PE is basically an ALU with a tiny register file, and PEs talk to their direct neighbours through a fixed interconnect. The difference from an FPGA is the granularity: FPGAs are reconfigured at the bit level, while CGRAs are reconfigured at the word level. Each cycle, every PE is told which operation to run and where to read its operands from. This makes CGRAs a good fit for compute-heavy kernels with regular data flow, because they give up some of the flexibility of an FPGA in exchange for better energy efficiency and much smaller configuration data.

\paragraph{Mapping process.}
\textit{Mapping} is the compilation step that decides which PE runs each operation and, if cycles are also considered, when. The input is normally a \textit{Data Flow Graph} (DFG): a directed acyclic graph where nodes are operations and edges are data dependencies. The mapper has to respect three kinds of constraints: \emph{operation compatibility} (a node can only go on a PE that supports its opcode), \emph{connectivity} (if $u \to v$ is a dependency, the PEs assigned to $u$ and $v$ must be directly connected, or the value has to be routed through other PEs or memory), and \emph{resource limits} (no PE runs more than one operation in the same cycle, fan-in/fan-out budgets are respected, and so on). Mapping is NP-hard in general, so the literature has explored both exact approaches (SAT/ILP-based) and heuristics.

\paragraph{Graph Neural Networks.}
A \textit{Graph Neural Network} (GNN) is a type of neural network that works directly on graphs. Each node starts with a feature vector and, through several rounds of message passing, mixes in information from its neighbours. After a few rounds, each node ends up with an embedding that captures both its own features and the structure around it, which can then be used to predict per-node, per-edge or per-graph properties. GNNs fit DFG-based problems naturally, because the input is already a graph and the things one usually wants to predict (scheduling priorities, distances, placement hints) live on the nodes.

\paragraph{LISA.}
LISA\cite{lisa2022} is a GNN-based approach for mapping DFGs onto spatial accelerators. Instead of trying to solve the mapping itself, the GNN is trained on traces from a reference mapper to predict a few labels per DFG node, such as scheduling order and spatial/temporal distances to dependent nodes. These labels are then handed to a downstream mapper as priorities or constraints, which nudges it towards solutions similar to the reference flow while staying portable across different accelerator targets.

\paragraph{SAT-MapIt.}
SAT-MapIt\cite{satmapit2023} is an exact, SAT-based mapper for CGRAs. It encodes the mapping problem as Boolean constraints over placement variables, and uses a \textit{Kernel Mobility Schedule} (KMS), a modulo-scheduling construct that bounds the cycles in which each operation can be issued, to shrink the search space before the SAT solver kicks in. The output is either a correct mapping (placement plus modulo schedule) or a proof that no mapping exists for the given resource budget.

In this project, LISA and SAT-MapIt are the two main references that the prototype combines: a LISA-style GNN provides per-node priorities, and a simplified, SAT-MapIt-inspired mapper checks each one-shot partition built from those priorities.

\paragraph{Related approaches.}
LISA is not the only learned approach to CGRA mapping. GRAFT\cite{10416096} predicts a placement distribution through GNN-based subgraph matching to steer a progressive graph-based mapper, reporting a $64.5\times$ mapping speedup over LISA with comparable or better mapping quality on CGRAs up to $16 \times 16$. Both LISA and GRAFT, however, assume the DFG fits within a single CGRA configuration and focus on accelerating placement and routing; neither addresses the partitioning problem that arises when this assumption does not hold. When a DFG exceeds the capacity of a single configuration, traditional approaches apply generic graph-partitioning algorithms such as the Kernighan-Lin heuristic or multi-level frameworks such as METIS\cite{karypis1998metis} to minimise edge cuts. These methods are agnostic to CGRA topology and routing constraints, so the partitions they produce are not guaranteed to be embeddable onto the target hardware. This project instead learns the partitioning step itself, predicting the partition structure prior to mapping.

\subsection{Goals and expected results}

The goals defined for the project were the following:

\begin{itemize}
	\item Study the state of the art on \textit{mapping} of DFGs to CGRAs, in particular GNN-based approaches inspired by LISA.
	\item Build a prototype that departs from current approaches by using GNNs to guide \textit{mapping} decisions.
	\item Evaluate the solution with end-to-end examples and contrast it with a purely algorithmic strategy.
\end{itemize}

flexibility to work on the project remotely and to communicate with my
supervisors and the rest of the team through a Slack\cite{slack} workspace

in which I presented what had been accomplished during the week and we

\subsection{Activities carried out}

The technical activities can be grouped into the following blocks:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{DFG representation}: definition of a JSON format for
	      nodes, operations, constants, metadata and dependency edges.
	\item \textbf{Example generation}: creation of synthetic graphs inspired
	      by matrix multiplication and convolution patterns.
	\item \textbf{Partitioning}: development of strategies to split
	      graphs that do not fit in a single CGRA configuration.
	\item \textbf{Materialisation}: conversion of inter-partition dependencies
	      into synthetic \textit{load}/\textit{store} nodes.
	\item \textbf{\textit{Mapping} validation}: SAT verification of each
	      materialised partition, respecting operation compatibility,
	      connectivity between PEs and resource limits.
	\item \textbf{LISA-GNN integration}: use of \textit{label0} predictions
	      as a priority for node selection in the greedy partitioner.
	\item \textbf{Evaluation}: execution of end-to-end examples and collection of
	      metrics on partitioning, cuts and SAT validation.
\end{itemize}

The initial phases (study, DFG generation,
partitioning) took place between the start of the semester and mid-March;
the LISA-GNN integration and the end-to-end pipeline were consolidated in May;
the evaluation and report writing followed in the final weeks.

The remainder of this section walks through the project's evolution
meeting by meeting, which is the most faithful way to describe how the
direction of the work was iteratively reshaped.

\subsubsection{First meeting (20 February 2026)}

The first meeting was used to re-introduce the team, agree on the
communication channel (Slack) and align on the immediate reading
material. I was tasked with surveying the state of the art on graph
\textit{mapping} for CGRAs, in particular GNN-based approaches, so that
the next meeting could be grounded on concrete papers rather than on the
generic proposal.

\subsubsection{Second meeting (27 February 2026)}

After the initial reading pass, the supervisors asked me to bring a
dataset of DFGs of the kind used in the state-of-the-art papers, to
review two or three of the most cited mappers, and to try out a
conventional mapper as a baseline. The discussion also opened two
questions that would stay with the project until the end: how to check
that a \textit{mapping} is valid, and how to represent a CGRA as a
graph. The recommendation was to keep both representations as simple as
possible.

\subsubsection{Third meeting (6 March 2026)}

By the third meeting I had cloned the LISA\cite{lisa2022} DFG generator
and produced a first synthetic dataset of graphs. Reproducing the LISA
flow end-to-end ran into a practical blocker: LISA itself relies on
\textit{Gurobi} on top of an \textit{LLVM} build, and the \textit{LLVM} dependency could not be
installed cleanly in the working environment. In parallel, I started
reading SAT-MapIt\cite{satmapit2023}, which uses a \textit{Kernel Mobility
	Schedule} (KMS), a construct from \textit{modulo scheduling} that bounds the
cycles in which each operation may be issued, to reduce the SAT search
space. The key idea that came out of this meeting was the one that ended up
defining the whole project: rather than picking one of the two
approaches, combine them. Use the LISA-style GNN to add constraints to
the graph, and let a SAT-based mapper consume those constraints.

\subsubsection{Fourth meeting (20 March 2026)}

Two things were settled in this meeting. First, the LISA flow was
reproduced for the homogeneous case: 1000 DFGs were generated for a
$4 \times 4$ CGRA and another 1000 for an $8 \times 8$ CGRA, although
mapping all of them with the reference flow turned out to be impractical
(roughly 40 graphs mapped in a full day of runtime for the $4 \times 4$
case). Second, I started reading \textit{NaviMap}, a GNN-plus-\textit{deep-Q-network}
mapping approach, and we discussed whether the LISA labels could be fed
into a NaviMap-like agent. The idea was left open as a possible third
leg of the prototype, to be evaluated in the following meeting after a
deeper read of the paper.

\subsubsection{Fifth meeting (27 March 2026)}

This meeting was when the NaviMap direction was dropped. After a closer
read, combining a \textit{deep-Q-network} agent on top of the GNN labels turned
out to add significant implementation and training complexity, having to
handle different input shapes for the Neural Networks as well as a lack of
open-source availability from NaviMap, without a clear advantage
over the simpler LISA-plus-SAT-MapIt pairing for the scale of graphs we
were targeting. The decision was therefore to set NaviMap aside and to
concentrate the remaining effort on the LISA-style labels combined with
the SAT mapper.

\subsubsection{Sixth meeting (10 April 2026)}

The discussion in this meeting was almost entirely about the
\textit{codegen} side of the project, led by Francisco. From my side,
the main takeaway was that the mapping flow had to produce a clean,
stable JSON output, so that the downstream code generation could
consume it without further negotiation about the format.

\subsubsection{Seventh meeting (17 April 2026)}

At this point I had a Python \texttt{CGRA} class that is passed to the
mapper, LISA was producing four labels per node (used as features), and
the experimental SAT path was using two of those labels (temporal and
spatial distance) on top of a \textit{CGRA-ME}-style template to produce
reference mappings for that prototype stage. The meeting surfaced a real, recurring problem: the DFG
formats used by LISA and by SAT-MapIt did not agree. LISA worked with a
small set of operations (\texttt{const}, \texttt{load}, \texttt{store},
\texttt{add}, \texttt{mul}, \texttt{sub}, \ldots), whereas SAT-MapIt
also reasoned about branches, \textit{live-ins}/\textit{live-outs} and other entities.
The action item was to consolidate a single input format and intermediate
JSON, under the assumption that everything is one-shot, and to check
whether SAT-MapIt could be coerced into a one-shot regime. This meeting was
when we decided to simplify the problem by focusing on one-shot mapping with no temporal constraints.

\subsubsection{Eighth meeting (24 April 2026)}

This is the meeting in which the eventual shape of the prototype was
decided. The problem on the table was that SAT-MapIt is built around
\textit{modulo scheduling}, its notion of an \textit{initiation
	interval}, and the way it places operations across cycles all assume
that regime, whereas LISA does not directly emit a SAT-MapIt KMS or a
complete modulo schedule. It predicts labels such as schedule order and
spatial/temporal distances that guide a downstream mapper. Plugging the LISA
labels straight into SAT-MapIt was therefore not straightforward. Due to time constraints and the scope of the Capstone Project,
the team decided to simplify and abstract the mapping into one-shot mapping. This would raise
an issue, where the LISA labels would be pointless since all the nodes would be place at timestep
0. My suggestion was to invert the approach: take a large DFG, run a learned model on it to obtain
per-node features, use those features to \emph{split} the graph in such
a way that cut dependencies are made explicit through synthetic memory
operations, and then one-shot
\textit{map} each subgraph with the SAT mapper. The open question
became how to perform the split itself, whether by a heuristic or by
another learned component.

\subsubsection{Ninth meeting (30 April 2026)}

The ninth meeting confirmed that the partitioned one-shot strategy
worked. A greedy partitioner was already in place, not yet driven by a
GNN, but by hand-crafted priorities such as \textit{ASAP}, \textit{ALAP} and edge count —
whose objective was to minimise the number of edges cut at the
partition boundary, and thus the amount of synthetic memory traffic
needed to keep memory operations outside of the CGRA's ALU array. The
splitting was working and each subgraph was being mapped one-shot with
the SAT-MapIt-inspired one-shot mapper to produce reference labels for
this abstraction. From here the plan was
clean: use the original large graph, augmented with the per-partition
timesteps as an extra feature, to train a GNN that predicts the
schedule order (\textit{label0}); then feed \textit{label0} into the
partitioner to obtain better partitions, and finally validate each
partition with the simplified SAT mapper. This is the flow that ended up being reported in this
document.


\section{Development of the solution}
\glsadd{dfg}\glsadd{cgra}\glsadd{pe}\glsadd{dag}\glsadd{sat}\glsadd{smt}\glsadd{z3}\glsadd{alu}\glsadd{oneshot}\glsadd{mapping}\glsadd{mapper}\glsadd{lisagnn}\glsadd{gnn}\glsadd{json}\glsadd{label0}\glsadd{ai}

\subsection{Requirements}

The project was always very open-ended in terms of the technical solution, as long as it
iterated on state of the art approaches using \textit{AI}. One technical constraint was to
agree on a JSON format for the output since another project for the codegen part was being
developed in parallel, and the mapping output had to be clean and stable for that project to consume it without further negotiation.

The prototype does not cover cycle-by-cycle temporal scheduling. The SAT
\textit{mapper} only verifies the spatial validity of each partition, that is,
it assumes that internal dependencies are resolved by direct connections
between PEs in the same configuration.

\subsection{Architecture and technologies}

The primary goal of the approach is to achieve the high partition quality of
exact mathematical solvers while maintaining the low computational overhead of
greedy heuristics. The solution was implemented in Python and organised as a
flow with two distinct phases, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:flow}: a training
phase, in which a GNN learns optimal partition structure from exhaustively
generated ground truth, and an inference phase, in which the trained GNN guides
the partitioning of unseen DFGs with SAT-based spatial validation.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{flow}
	\caption{Overview of the proposed flow. The methodology is divided into
		two distinct phases: training the GNN, and inference-time graph
		partitioning with SAT-based spatial validation.}
	\label{fig:flow}
\end{figure}

The flow comprises three stages: (1) synthetic graph generation, (2)
ground-truth generation and GNN training, and (3) GNN-guided partitioning and
spatial mapping. Stages (1) and (2) constitute the GNN training phase, and
stage (3) represents the DFG partitioning inference.

In summary, a GNN is trained to predict, for each instruction node of an input
DFG, the partition it should belong to in an optimal split, that is, a split
which minimises the inter-partition cut edges while maintaining spatial
mappability on the target CGRA. To obtain a ground truth relating mapping
quality to graph splits, an exhaustive \textsc{SAT-min-k} search is performed
per training graph: every possible split is attempted and its mapping quality is
recorded against the chosen split. The trained GNN then predicts, for an input
test DFG, the best partition to which each node should belong, and adds this
information as attributes on the nodes. A topology-aware partitioner processes
the annotated graph, performing a final refinement to ensure mapping validity
given the CGRA's hardware constraints. By reducing the mapping search space to
an initial inferred solution, the DFG mapping time is reduced. Each partition is
finally verified by a SAT mapper implemented with Z3\cite{z3} and exported as a
PE configuration.

\subsubsection{DFG model}

A DFG is modelled as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) in which the nodes
represent operations and the edges represent data dependencies. Constants are
root nodes and memory operations are represented explicitly through
\texttt{load} and \texttt{store}. In the current model, all edges have zero
distance, that is, a dependency inside a partition must be satisfied in the
same one-shot configuration. The node types are drawn from a set of ten
operations: arithmetic (\texttt{add}, \texttt{sub}, \texttt{mul}), bitwise
logic (\texttt{and}, \texttt{or}, \texttt{xor}), shifts (\texttt{shl},
\texttt{shr}), and memory operations (\texttt{load}, \texttt{store}). Immediate
constants are modelled as dedicated constant nodes with a value sampled
uniformly from $[-256, 256]$.

\subsubsection{Synthetic graph generation}

Training a GNN-based mapper requires a large corpus of labelled DFGs that span a
representative range of kernel shapes, data-dependency structures and
memory-access patterns, while remaining within the resource bounds of the
target CGRA. Because hand-written or compiler-extracted benchmarks are too few
and too uniform for this purpose, a parametric generator was built that produces
synthetic DFGs by starting from a small kernel template and applying a sequence
of structure-preserving mutations.

Two loop-body templates seed the generator. The \emph{matrix-multiplication}
template encodes the inner accumulation $c \mathrel{+}= a[k] \times b[k]$ as a
six-node DAG: two loads feeding a \texttt{mul}, whose result is added to a third
load and then written back by a \texttt{store}. The \emph{convolution} template
encodes $y \mathrel{+}= w[k] \times (x[k] \mathrel{\ll} 2)$ and extends this
chain with a constant node and a \texttt{shl} operation, yielding an eight-node
seed. Both templates are acyclic, represent one inner loop iteration, have
exactly one memory write, and satisfy all CGRA structural invariants by
construction.

To increase graph variability, the generator applies a sequence of graph
mutations over the templates, partitioned into three structural classes:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \emph{Depth mutations} lengthen the critical path by inserting
	      operations along existing producer-consumer chains, increasing
	      pipeline depth.
	\item \emph{Breadth mutations} introduce new parallel computation by
	      branching live values into independent paths, merging independent
	      values through binary operations, or adding fan-out to existing nodes
	      without extending depth. These mutations increase ASAP-level width and
	      routing pressure.
	\item \emph{Neutral mutations} alter operations or operand types without
	      changing graph topology, replacing nodes with others of the same type,
	      or replacing a memory load with an immediate constant operand. These
	      mutations increase operation-type diversity without affecting the
	      graph's structural complexity.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{CGRA model}

The CGRA is described by a grid of PEs. Each PE can support a subset of
operations and, in some architectures, only some PEs can perform memory
operations. The topology defines which PEs are directly connected. In the
current experiments a homogeneous $4 \times 4$ CGRA was used. Figure~\ref{fig:cgra-4x4} shows the canonical layout we
assume: a 2D grid of PEs, with data and instruction memories on the
side, where each PE is essentially an \textit{ALU} with a local register file
and can operate on the results delivered by its neighbouring PEs.

\subsubsection{One-shot mapping validity}

For a partition to be considered mappable, a custom SAT placement encoding,
distinct from the modulo-scheduling formulation of SAT-MapIt\cite{satmapit2023},
must find an assignment of nodes to PEs that satisfies the following conditions:

Let $V_I$ be the set of instruction nodes of the partition, $E_I$ the set of
instruction-to-instruction edges, and $\mathcal{E}$ the set of PEs of the CGRA.
For every $(v,e) \in V_I \times \mathcal{E}$ we introduce a Boolean placement
variable
\[
	p_{v,e} \in \{0,1\}, \qquad p_{v,e} = 1 \iff \text{node } v
	\text{ is placed on PE } e.
\]
The mapping is then encoded by the following clauses:

\paragraph{(C1) Operation compatibility.}
Let $\mathit{op}(v)$ denote the opcode of node $v$ and $\mathit{ops}(e)$
the set of opcodes supported by PE $e$. For each node $v$ and each PE
$e$ that does not support $\mathit{op}(v)$, the placement is forbidden:
\begin{equation}
	\forall v \in V_I,\ \forall e \in \mathcal{E},\
	\mathit{op}(v) \notin \mathit{ops}(e) \implies \neg p_{v,e}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{(C2) Exactly one PE per instruction.}
Each instruction node is placed on exactly one PE, enforced as a
pseudo-Boolean equality:
\begin{equation}
	\forall v \in V_I,\ \sum_{e \in \mathcal{E}} p_{v,e} = 1.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{(C3) At most one instruction per PE.}
Each PE may host at most one instruction of the partition:
\begin{equation}
	\forall e \in \mathcal{E},\ \sum_{v \in V_I} p_{v,e} \leq 1.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{(C4) Same-cycle nearest-neighbour dependencies.}
Let $\mathit{nbr}(a,b)$ denote that PEs $a$ and $b$ are directly
connected in the CGRA topology (or that $a = b$ when the model allows
intra-PE forwarding). For every internal dependency
$u \rightarrow v \in E_I$, at least one feasible neighbouring assignment
must be selected:
\begin{equation}
	\forall (u,v) \in E_I,\
	\bigvee_{\substack{a,b \in \mathcal{E} \\ \mathit{nbr}(a,b)}}
	\bigl( p_{u,a} \wedge p_{v,b} \bigr).
\end{equation}
This is a simplification of SAT-MapIt's temporal forward-edge
constraint: dependent instructions may share the same partition (and
therefore the same one-shot cycle) as long as their PEs are physically
adjacent.

\paragraph{(C5) Memory-capable PEs.}
For each memory node $v \in N_{\mathit{mem}}$ (where
$N_{\mathit{mem}} = V_{\text{load}} \cup V_{\text{store}}$) and each PE
$e$ that does not provide memory access ($e \notin \mathit{mem}$):
\begin{equation}
	\forall v \in N_{\mathit{mem}},\ \forall e \in \mathcal{E},\
	e \notin \mathit{mem} \implies \neg p_{v,e}.
\end{equation}
On the homogeneous target evaluated here every PE supports every operation and
every PE has data-memory access, so $\mathit{mem} = \mathcal{E}$ and (C5) is
inactive; it is retained for heterogeneous architectures with a memory-capable
PE subset.

\paragraph{Post-solution resource checks.}
Fan-in, fan-out and physical-link capacity are not encoded in the SAT
formulation. Once Z3 returns a model, the prototype reads back the
placements and rejects any solution in which, for the chosen limits, a
PE exceeds its outgoing or incoming edge budget, or a physical link
between two PEs is used by more edges than its capacity. A violation
here is reported back to the caller as an infeasible mapping rather
than as a SAT failure.

A structure such as $A \rightarrow C \leftarrow B$ is valid within a single
partition if $A$, $B$ and $C$ are placed on compatible PEs and if the PEs of
$A$ and $B$ can deliver their results directly to the PE of $C$, within the
limits of $C$'s inputs and the physical links. It should be noted that the
prototype covers spatial feasibility inside the partition, not temporal
scheduling.

\subsubsection{DFG partitioning}

When the input finite DFG does not fit in a single CGRA configuration, the graph is
split into a sequence of partitions $P_1, \ldots, P_K$. Because partitions
execute sequentially, a value can only be read from an equal-or-earlier
partition, so to preserve the order of the dependencies the partitioner enforces:

\begin{equation}
	partition(src) \leq partition(dest)
\end{equation}

for each edge $src \rightarrow dest$. This constraint prevents dependencies
that would have to flow back to a previous partition. Only such \emph{monotone}
assignments, which respect the topological order of the graph, are considered.

\subsubsection{Ground-truth generation}

After generating a set of DFGs, a training dataset for the GNN is built by
systematic partition and mapping to valid solutions. A valid solution is defined
by partitioning the DFG nodes into $K$ non-empty subsets. When edges are cut
between these subsets they are transformed into synthetic \texttt{load}/%
\texttt{store} pairs, and each resulting subset must then be successfully mapped.

The objective of generating ground truth is to find the partition assignment
$\sigma^*$ that jointly minimises the number of partitions $K$ and the number of
cut edges, subject to CGRA constraints. Once $\sigma^*$ is found, each
instruction node $v \in V_I$ is labelled with its assigned partition index
$\sigma^*(v)$, which serves as the ground-truth target for the GNN's
multi-class classification.

To find this assignment, an exhaustive \textsc{SAT-min-k} search
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:sat-min-k}) is performed over each synthetic DFG. The
algorithm performs an upper-bounded sweep over $K$, starting from a
resource-derived lower bound $K_{\min}$ (the instruction count $|V_I|$ divided by
the number of PEs) up to $|V_I|$, the trivial case in which each partition
contains a single instruction. For each value of $K$, all valid monotone
assignments are enumerated exhaustively and SAT-checked, guaranteeing that the
returned partition is minimal in $K$ and optimal in edge cuts among all feasible
$K$-partition solutions.

Each feasible partition is scored by the tuple:

\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{S}(\mathcal{P}) = \bigl(\,|\text{cut}(\mathcal{P})|,\; |V_{\text{syn}}|,\; \max_i f_i,\; \max_i \rho_i,\; \max_i \ell_i\,\bigr)
	\label{eq:score}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $|\text{cut}(\mathcal{P})|$ is the number of cut edges,
$|V_{\text{syn}}|$ the number of data transfers introduced by partitioning,
$\max_i f_i$ the maximum frontier edge count across partitions, $\max_i \rho_i$
the maximum PE occupancy, and $\max_i \ell_i$ the maximum physical link
utilisation. Candidates are ranked lexicographically by $\mathcal{S}$, so cut
minimisation is the primary objective and the remaining terms act as
tiebreakers in order of priority. Among all feasible $K$-partition candidates,
the one minimising $\mathcal{S}$ is selected as the ground-truth solution for
that graph.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
	\caption{\textsc{SAT-min-k} Partitioning}\label{alg:sat-min-k}
	\begin{algorithmic}
		\Require DFG $G = (V, E)$, CGRA architecture $\mathcal{A}$
		\Comment{$V_I \subseteq V$: instruction nodes}
		\Ensure Minimum-$K$ feasible partition $\mathcal{P}^*$ with minimum edge cuts
		\State $K_{\min} \leftarrow \lceil |V_I| / \text{capacity}(\mathcal{A}) \rceil$ \Comment{Resource lower bound}
		\For{$K \in \{K_{\min}, \ldots, |V_I|\}$}
		\For{each monotone assignment $\sigma : V_I \to [K]$ of instruction nodes to $K$ non-empty partitions}
		\State Prune $\sigma$ if materialised resource usage is infeasible \Comment{Early rejection}
		\State Deduplicate $\sigma$ against memoised isomorphic partitions \Comment{Cache Hit}
		\State Construct partitions $\mathcal{P} = \{P_1, \ldots, P_K\}$ from $\sigma$
		\State Materialise cut edges as synthetic \textit{load}/\textit{store} pairs
		\If{$\mathcal{P}$ is structurally infeasible (routing or capacity)}
		\State \textbf{continue}
		\EndIf
		\If{\textsc{SAT-based spatial mapper}($P_i$, $\mathcal{A}$) is feasible $\forall i \in [K]$}
		\If{$\mathcal{S}(\mathcal{P}) < \mathcal{S}(\mathcal{P}^*)$}
		\State $\mathcal{P}^* \leftarrow \mathcal{P}$ \Comment{lexicographic min, Eq.~\eqref{eq:score}}
		\EndIf
		\EndIf
		\EndFor
		\If{$\mathcal{P}^*$ is defined}
		\State \Return $\mathcal{P}^*$ \Comment{Optimal $K$-partition; do not increment $K$}
		\EndIf
		\EndFor
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

This exhaustive search yields provably optimal partition assignments, but its
cost grows quickly with graph size, which is precisely why it is used only
offline to generate ground truth rather than at inference time. A faster greedy
partitioner, which builds a sequence of partitions through a single pass over
the ready nodes, was also implemented as a baseline.

\subsubsection{Materialisation of inter-partition dependencies}

An edge that crosses a partition boundary cannot be represented as a direct
connection between PEs, because the two operations belong to distinct
configurations. For a non-load producer, the cut edge $u \rightarrow v$ is
materialised as:

\begin{center}
	\texttt{P\_i: u -> synthetic\_store}
	\hspace{1cm}
	\texttt{P\_j: synthetic\_load -> v}
\end{center}

The two synthetic nodes share the same logical memory reference. When the
producer is an original \texttt{load}, the destination partition reloads the
same original memory reference and the source-side synthetic store is omitted.
The overall
approach is therefore a \textbf{partitioned one-shot mapping}: temporal
decomposition between partitions, spatial \textit{mapping} within each
partition.

\subsubsection{GNN training}

The ground-truth label assigned to each instruction node $v \in V_I$ is its
partition index $\sigma^*(v) \in \{0, \ldots, K^*-1\}$ under the selected
assignment $\sigma^*$, where $K^*$ is the minimum feasible $K$ returned by
\textsc{SAT-min-k} (Algorithm~\ref{alg:sat-min-k}). Each node is encoded with
the feature set introduced by LISA\cite{lisa2022}. The model is trained with
inverse-frequency-weighted cross-entropy loss to compensate for the class
imbalance arising from variable partition sizes across training graphs:

\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{L} = -\sum_{v \in V_I} w_{\sigma^*(v)} \log \hat{p}\bigl(\sigma^*(v) \mid v\bigr)
	\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\hat{p}(\cdot \mid v)$ is the softmax output of the classifier
for node $v$ and $w_k \propto 1/|\{v : \sigma^*(v) = k\}|$ is the inverse class
frequency weight for partition $k$.

\subsubsection{GNN-guided partitioning and repair}

At inference time, the trained GNN, a multi-layer GraphSAGE\cite{hamilton2017inductive}
classifier, assigns each node $v \in V_I$ of an unseen DFG an initial partition
index $\hat{\sigma}(v)$ (its \emph{seed}). These seeds do not directly guarantee
mappable partitions, so a topology-aware partitioner refines them into a feasible
one.

\paragraph{Feasibility.}
Each node carries an integer partition index, and the set of nodes sharing an
index forms one partition; changing a node's index therefore moves it between
partitions. A partition $P_i$ is \emph{feasible} when its materialised subgraph,
including the synthetic \texttt{load}/\texttt{store} nodes introduced for cut
edges, satisfies all of:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item node count, operation mix and memory port usage are within the
	      resource caps of the target CGRA;
	\item the number of outgoing cut edges does not exceed the configured
	      frontier budget;
	\item the materialised partition passes structural pre-SAT filters;
	\item the materialised partition is accepted by the SAT-based spatial mapper.
\end{enumerate}
A partitioning is feasible when every partition is feasible. \emph{Repair} is
the procedure that turns the raw GNN seeding into a feasible partitioning by
adjusting the partition indices and, where necessary, splitting partitions.

\paragraph{Guided partitioning and repair.}
Rather than performing a conventional list-scheduling sweep, the partitioner
takes all seeds $\hat{\sigma}$ at once and repairs them. It first makes the
indices order-consistent: because partitions execute sequentially, a partition
must be \emph{convex} (topologically contiguous), that is, no partition may hold
a consumer placed before its producer. Each node is therefore pushed to at least
one index beyond its latest instruction-valued predecessor:
\begin{equation}
	\pi(v) = \max\!\left(\hat{\sigma}(v),\;
	\max_{(p \to v) \in E_I} \pi(p) + 1\right)
	\label{eq:convex}
\end{equation}
Memory loads, which have no instruction-valued predecessor, are then pulled
forward to their earliest consumer so they share a partition with their first
use:
\begin{equation}
	\pi(\ell) = \min_{(\ell \to s) \in E_I}\, \pi(s)
	\qquad \forall\, \ell \in V_{\text{load}}
	\label{eq:load-pull}
\end{equation}
Convexification can leave empty indices; since an empty index would correspond
to an empty, no-op configuration, the indices are compacted to a dense range
$\{0, \ldots, K-1\}$, after which $K$ is the actual number of partitions.

These partitions are then checked and repaired in index order, processed as a
queue. A feasible partition is finalised immediately. An infeasible partition
containing more than one node is split at its largest feasible topological
prefix: the prefix is finalised, a synthetic \texttt{store}/\texttt{load} pair is
materialised at the cut to represent the introduced cut edge, and the remaining
suffix is re-enqueued at the front of the queue as a new candidate. Because a
single instruction node is always mappable onto the target CGRA, this converges
to a feasible partitioning, at the cost of at most one additional cut edge per
split. The loop is bounded by a permissive safeguard of $4|V_I| + 16$
iterations; exceeding it raises a partitioning error.

\paragraph{Spatial mapping.}
Once partitioning is complete, each feasible partition $P_i$ is mapped
independently onto the CGRA by the SAT-based spatial mapper, with one SAT solve
per partition. Since every partition is capacity-feasible by construction, each
solve operates on a subgraph that fits within the CGRA's spatial resources,
keeping solve time tractable independently of the size of the original DFG. The
GNN does not replace the solver: the validity of the mapping still depends on the
structural constraints and the SAT verification.

\subsection{Developed solution}

The developed solution is a Python pipeline that takes a DFG and a CGRA
description as input and produces, for each one-shot partition of the
graph, a SAT-validated placement of the partition's instructions onto
the PEs of the CGRA. The pipeline is organised in five stages, each
implemented as an independent module and exercised by the same set of
end-to-end tests:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{DFG generation.} Either a synthetic graph is produced
	      by the in-tree generator (\texttt{dfg\_gen}), or an existing
	      JSON DFG is loaded from disk. The format follows the model
	      described in Section~3.2.1 — instruction nodes, constant nodes
	      and \texttt{load}/\texttt{store} memory nodes, with zero-distance
	      data dependency edges.
	\item \textbf{GNN inference.} The trained GraphSAGE classifier reads
	      the DFG and produces a per-node partition index
	      $\hat{\sigma}(v)$, used downstream as a seed by the topology-aware
	      partitioner.
	\item \textbf{Partitioning.} The DFG is split into an ordered
	      sequence $P_1, \ldots, P_K$ of subgraphs that respect the
	      topological order of the original graph. The topology-aware
	      partitioner seeds the layout from the GNN predictions and repairs
	      it into a feasible partitioning through convexification and
	      capacity repair. The offline \textsc{SAT-min-k} search, which
	      probes increasing values of $K$ and validates each candidate via
	      SAT to prove the smallest feasible $K$, is used to generate
	      ground truth rather than at inference time.
	\item \textbf{Materialisation.} Cross-partition dependencies are
	      rewritten as synthetic memory operations: non-load producers use
	      paired synthetic \texttt{store}/\texttt{load} nodes, while cuts
	      from original loads are represented as reloads in the destination
	      partition. This yields one self-contained DFG per partition.
	\item \textbf{SAT mapping.} Each materialised partition is handed to
	      the SAT mapper described in Section~3.2.3, which either
	      returns a valid placement or rejects the partition.
\end{enumerate}

The resulting artefacts are a sequence of per-partition JSON mappings,
each one listing the PE assignment of every node together with the
operand wiring (PE-to-PE links, immediate constants and memory
references). This is the input consumed downstream by the code
generation work developed in parallel by the team colleague.

\subsection{Validation}

\subsubsection{Experimental setup}

$4 \times 4$ homogeneous CGRA was targeted, as described in
Section~3.2.2 and shown in Figure~\ref{fig:cgra-4x4}.

Five partitioning strategies were evaluated to characterise the contribution of
each component of the framework. \textsc{SAT-min-k} (Algorithm~\ref{alg:sat-min-k})
serves as the optimal reference, performing an exhaustive search over all valid
$K$-partition assignments and returning the minimum-cut solution at the smallest
feasible $K$. A greedy list-scheduling partitioner is included as a fast
baseline, building partitions in a single irreversible sweep without searching
over alternative assignments, representative of conventional compilation
heuristics. To isolate the contribution of the guided partitioner independently
of the GNN, the topological strategy runs the topology-aware partitioner with no
learned input: all nodes are seeded to partition $0$ and boundaries are
determined entirely by the convexification and capacity repair steps. The
\textsc{GNN-raw} strategy takes per-node partition indices predicted by the GNN
and constructs partitions directly from these assignments without any
convexification or feasibility repair, submitting them to the SAT-based spatial
mapper as-is; this isolates the raw predictive quality of the GNN from the
contribution of the repair procedure. Finally, the full \textsc{GNN-guided}
strategy seeds the topology-aware partitioner with GNN predictions, combining
learned partition structure with topology-aware repair, and constitutes the
primary contribution of this work.

The framework operates under two simplifying assumptions. Each partition is
assumed to complete execution within a single CGRA configuration cycle,
consistent with the one-shot mapping model of the SAT-based spatial mapper and a
common assumption in spatial CGRA compilation; it does not account for
multi-cycle operations or data-dependent latencies that would require a modulo
scheduling pass. Cut edges are materialised as synthetic \texttt{store}/%
\texttt{load} pairs at partition boundaries, implying a shared data memory for
transferring live values between consecutive configurations. The framework does
not model transfer latency or bandwidth, nor architectures using dedicated
inter-configuration registers or FIFOs. The number of cut edges serves as a
proxy for inter-partition communication overhead under the assumption that each
synthetic memory pair incurs a uniform cost.

(Table~\ref{tab:benchmark_common}) removes the bias: the optimum falls to
$1.09$, and \textsc{GNN-guided} ($1.60$ cuts) produces \emph{fewer} cuts than
\textsc{GNN-raw} ($1.74$), closing $73\%$ of the gap to the optimum versus $66\%$
for the raw predictions. The repair procedure therefore improves partition
quality on top of restoring feasibility, rather than degrading it; the apparent
advantage of raw predictions on the full set was an artefact of their
restriction to easier graphs.

\paragraph{Runtime.}
\textsc{GNN-raw} is the fastest non-trivial strategy: partitioning takes
$0.4$\,ms and SAT mapping $0.07$\,s, matching greedy's per-partition cost since
both submit exactly one SAT check per partition. \textsc{GNN-guided}
(partitioning $4$\,ms, SAT mapping $0.24$\,s) is approximately $50\times$ faster
end-to-end than \textsc{SAT-min-k} (Algorithm~\ref{alg:sat-min-k}) ($12.1$\,s
total), while the topological strategy ($0.50$\,s SAT mapping) is slower than
\textsc{GNN-guided} despite producing worse partitions, because a poor seed
drives more feasibility-repair iterations and consequently more SAT checks per
partition ($3.71$ vs.\ $2.36$).


\section{Conclusions}
\glsadd{sdg}\glsadd{cgra}

\subsection{Results achieved}

Beyond the prototype itself, the work culminated in a scientific communication
that will be published at INForum 2026\footnote{\url{https://inforum.pt/}}, the
Portuguese symposium on informatics, consolidating the framework and its
evaluation into a contribution.

This work produced a GNN-guided partitioning framework for spatial CGRA mapping
that learns optimal partition structure from exhaustive SAT-generated ground
truth. The flow addresses the case where the DFG does not fit within the CGRA:
while most approaches focus on accelerating placement and routing, this work
targets the pre-mapping partitioning phase. The framework makes three concrete
contributions. First, a configurable synthetic DFG generator, which produces
training graphs by applying structure-preserving mutations to kernel templates,
enabling large-scale dataset generation without relying on compiler-extracted
benchmarks. Second, an exhaustive \textsc{SAT-min-k} search
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:sat-min-k}) that provides provably optimal partition
assignments as ground-truth labels, ensuring the GNN learns from high-quality
supervision. Third, a topology-aware guided partitioner that translates per-node
GNN predictions into feasible partitions through convexification and capacity
repair.

Evaluation on a $4 \times 4$ homogeneous CGRA with five strategies shows that
the \textsc{GNN-guided} flow is the strongest practical method. On the full
evaluation set it achieves a $98\%$ feasibility rate and reduces mean edge cuts
by $33\%$ relative to the unguided topological baseline, closing $55\%$ of the
gap to the \textsc{SAT-min-k} optimum at roughly $50\times$ lower runtime. A
like-for-like comparison on the $106$ graphs that \textsc{GNN-raw} also solves
(Table~\ref{tab:benchmark_common}) clarifies the role of the repair procedure:
once graph structure is controlled for, \textsc{GNN-guided} produces fewer cuts
than \textsc{GNN-raw} ($1.60$ vs.\ $1.74$, against an optimum of $1.09$), closing
$73\%$ of the gap to the optimum versus $66\%$ for the raw predictions. The
apparent quality advantage of \textsc{GNN-raw} on the full set was therefore an
artefact of its restriction to structurally easier graphs, not evidence that raw
seeds are superior; repair both restores feasibility ($98\%$ vs.\ $70.7\%$) and
lowers the cut count. The large gap between the unguided topological baseline
($3.00$ cuts on the common subset) and both GNN-seeded variants confirms that the
learned predictions carry strong partition structure, and the residual gap to the
optimum reflects prediction error rather than a limitation of the repair
mechanism.

\subsection{Lessons learned}

The most important lesson of the internship was that, in a research project, the
problem statement is itself part of the work. The pipeline reported here is not
the one envisioned at the start: it is the result of several rounds of
simplification negotiated in the weekly meetings. Reproducing the LISA flow
end-to-end was blocked by an \textit{LLVM} build dependency, the NaviMap
direction was investigated and then dropped once its implementation and training
cost proved disproportionate to its benefit at our scale, and the eventual
decision to abstract the problem into one-shot partitioned mapping only emerged
once it became clear that plugging LISA labels directly into a modulo-scheduling
mapper such as SAT-MapIt was not straightforward. Committing early to a stable
JSON interface with the parallel code-generation work also proved valuable, as it
let the two efforts proceed without renegotiating the format. The recurring
theme was that scoping the problem to match the available time, while keeping the
core research idea intact, was as decisive as any single implementation choice.

\subsection{Future work}

Several directions remain open. The current evaluation targets a $4 \times 4$
homogeneous CGRA; scaling to larger grids and heterogeneous architectures, where
PEs support different ISA subsets, will stress both the GNN's generalisation and
the repair procedure's routing assumptions. An alternative GNN formulation
predicting cut probability per edge in addition to partition index per node could
close the remaining quality gap more directly. Finally, the framework currently
assumes single-cycle, one-shot execution per partition; integrating temporal or
modulo scheduling would substantially broaden its applicability to real
compilation workflows.

\subsection{SDG impact}

The project aligns with SDG 9, Industry, Innovation and Infrastructure, by
investigating compilation and optimisation techniques for reconfigurable
architectures. More efficient mappings for CGRAs can reduce the energy
consumption of computational workloads accelerated on this type of hardware,
even though that gain was not quantified in this work.
