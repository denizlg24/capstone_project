\section{Introduction}
\glsadd{ai}\glsadd{gnn}\glsadd{dfg}\glsadd{cgra}\glsadd{feup}\glsadd{inesctec}\glsadd{lisa}\glsadd{lisagnn}\glsadd{sat}

\subsection{Context}

This report documents the work carried out in the Capstone Project course unit, part of the Bachelor's in Informatics and Computing Engineering at the Faculty of Engineering of the University of Porto (FEUP), during the second semester of the 2025/2026 academic year.

The project was developed at \textbf{INESC TEC}, under the supervision of Professor Nuno Paulino and the tutorship of Professor João Cardoso. The goal was to investigate and implement an approach inspired by LISA\cite{lisa2022}, based on \textit{Graph Neural Networks} (GNNs), for the \textit{mapping} of \textit{Data Flow Graphs} (DFGs) onto \textit{Coarse-Grained Reconfigurable Architectures} (CGRAs).

INESC TEC is an associate laboratory active in engineering and computer science, hosting applied research in compilation and reconfigurable architectures, which are the areas in which this project is positioned.



\subsection{Goals and expected results}

The goals defined for the project were the following:

\begin{itemize}
	\item Study the state of the art on \textit{mapping} of DFGs to CGRAs, in particular GNN-based approaches inspired by LISA.
	\item Build a prototype that departs from current approaches by using GNNs to guide \textit{mapping} decisions.
	\item Evaluate the solution with end-to-end examples and contrast it with a purely algorithmic strategy.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Report structure}

The next section frames the methodology, stakeholders and activities. The
development section describes the DFG model, the CGRA model, the
partitioning, the materialisation of dependencies between partitions, the
SAT validation and the integration of the LISA-GNN. The experimental
validation, known limitations and future work follow.

This project was conducted at INESC TEC in a hybrid model. I had the
flexibility to work on the project remotely and to communicate with my
supervisors and the rest of the team through a Slack\cite{slack} workspace
that belongs to the team and to which I was invited.

Throughout the process, weekly meetings were held in the INESC TEC room
in which I presented what had been accomplished during the week and we
discussed the results and what should be addressed in the following one.
For these meetings I prepared a short presentation of about 5-10 minutes.
These meetings were an opportunity not only to clarify questions about the

\subsection{Activities carried out}

The technical activities can be grouped into the following blocks:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{DFG representation}: definition of a JSON format for
	      nodes, operations, constants, metadata and dependency edges.
	\item \textbf{Example generation}: creation of synthetic graphs inspired
	      by matrix multiplication and convolution patterns.
	\item \textbf{Partitioning}: development of strategies to split
	      graphs that do not fit in a single CGRA configuration.
	\item \textbf{Materialisation}: conversion of inter-partition dependencies
	      into synthetic \textit{load}/\textit{store} nodes.
	\item \textbf{\textit{Mapping} validation}: SAT verification of each
	      materialised partition, respecting operation compatibility,
	      connectivity between PEs and resource limits.
	\item \textbf{LISA-GNN integration}: use of \textit{label0} predictions
	      as a priority for node selection in the greedy partitioner.
	\item \textbf{Evaluation}: execution of end-to-end examples and collection of
	      metrics on partitioning, cuts and SAT validation.
\end{itemize}

The initial phases (study, DFG generation,
partitioning) took place between the start of the semester and mid-March;
the LISA-GNN integration and the end-to-end pipeline were consolidated in May;
the evaluation and report writing followed in the final weeks.

The remainder of this section walks through the project's evolution
meeting by meeting, which is the most faithful way to describe how the
direction of the work was iteratively reshaped.

\subsubsection{First meeting (20 February 2026)}

The first meeting was used to re-introduce the team, agree on the
communication channel (Slack) and align on the immediate reading
material. I was tasked with surveying the state of the art on graph
\textit{mapping} for CGRAs, in particular GNN-based approaches, so that
the next meeting could be grounded on concrete papers rather than on the
generic proposal.

\subsubsection{Second meeting (27 February 2026)}

After the initial reading pass, the supervisors asked me to bring a
dataset of DFGs of the kind used in the state-of-the-art papers, to
review two or three of the most cited mappers, and to try out a
conventional mapper as a baseline. The discussion also opened two
questions that would stay with the project until the end: how to check
that a \textit{mapping} is valid, and how to represent a CGRA as a
graph. The recommendation was to keep both representations as simple as
possible.

\subsubsection{Third meeting (6 March 2026)}

By the third meeting I had cloned the LISA\cite{lisa2022} DFG generator
and produced a first synthetic dataset of graphs. Reproducing the LISA
flow end-to-end ran into a practical blocker: LISA itself relies on
\textit{Gurobi} on top of an \textit{LLVM} build, and the \textit{LLVM} dependency could not be
installed cleanly in the working environment. In parallel, I started
reading SAT-MapIt\cite{satmapit2023}, which uses a \textit{Kernel Mobility
	Schedule} (KMS), a construct from \textit{modulo scheduling} that bounds the
cycles in which each operation may be issued, to reduce the SAT search
space. The key idea that came out of this meeting was the one that ended up
defining the whole project: rather than picking one of the two
approaches, combine them. Use the LISA-style GNN to add constraints to
the graph, and let a SAT-based mapper consume those constraints.

\subsubsection{Fourth meeting (20 March 2026)}

Two things were settled in this meeting. First, the LISA flow was
reproduced for the homogeneous case: 1000 DFGs were generated for a
$4 \times 4$ CGRA and another 1000 for an $8 \times 8$ CGRA, although
mapping all of them with the reference flow turned out to be impractical
(roughly 40 graphs mapped in a full day of runtime for the $4 \times 4$
case). Second, I started reading \textit{NaviMap}, a GNN-plus-\textit{deep-Q-network}
mapping approach, and we discussed whether the LISA labels could be fed
into a NaviMap-like agent. The idea was left open as a possible third
leg of the prototype, to be evaluated in the following meeting after a
deeper read of the paper.

\subsubsection{Fifth meeting (27 March 2026)}

This meeting was when the NaviMap direction was dropped. After a closer
read, combining a \textit{deep-Q-network} agent on top of the GNN labels turned
out to add significant implementation and training complexity, having to
handle different input shapes for the Neural Networks as well as a lack of
open-source availability from NaviMap, without a clear advantage
over the simpler LISA-plus-SAT-MapIt pairing for the scale of graphs we
were targeting. The decision was therefore to set NaviMap aside and to
concentrate the remaining effort on the LISA-style labels combined with
the SAT mapper.

\subsubsection{Sixth meeting (10 April 2026)}

The discussion in this meeting was almost entirely about the
\textit{codegen} side of the project, led by Francisco. From my side,
the main takeaway was that the mapping flow had to produce a clean,
stable JSON output, so that the downstream code generation could
consume it without further negotiation about the format.

\subsubsection{Seventh meeting (17 April 2026)}

At this point I had a Python \texttt{CGRA} class that is passed to the
mapper, LISA was producing four labels per node (used as features), and
the experimental SAT path was using two of those labels (temporal and
spatial distance) on top of a \textit{CGRA-ME}-style template to produce
reference mappings for that prototype stage. The meeting surfaced a real, recurring problem: the DFG
formats used by LISA and by SAT-MapIt did not agree. LISA worked with a
small set of operations (\texttt{const}, \texttt{load}, \texttt{store},
\texttt{add}, \texttt{mul}, \texttt{sub}, \ldots), whereas SAT-MapIt
also reasoned about branches, \textit{live-ins}/\textit{live-outs} and other entities.
The action item was to consolidate a single input format and intermediate
JSON, under the assumption that everything is one-shot, and to check
whether SAT-MapIt could be coerced into a one-shot regime. This meeting was
when we decided to simplify the problem by focusing on one-shot mapping with no temporal constraints.

\subsubsection{Eighth meeting (24 April 2026)}

This is the meeting in which the eventual shape of the prototype was
decided. The problem on the table was that SAT-MapIt is built around
\textit{modulo scheduling}, its notion of an \textit{initiation
	interval}, and the way it places operations across cycles all assume
that regime, whereas LISA does not directly emit a SAT-MapIt KMS or a
complete modulo schedule. It predicts labels such as schedule order and
spatial/temporal distances that guide a downstream mapper. Plugging the LISA
labels straight into SAT-MapIt was therefore not straightforward. Due to time constraints and the scope of the Capstone Project,
the team decided to simplify and abstract the mapping into one-shot mapping. This would raise
an issue, where the LISA labels would be pointless since all the nodes would be place at timestep
0. My suggestion was to invert the approach: take a large DFG, run a learned model on it to obtain
per-node features, use those features to \emph{split} the graph in such
a way that cut dependencies are made explicit through synthetic memory
operations, and then one-shot
\textit{map} each subgraph with the SAT mapper. The open question
became how to perform the split itself, whether by a heuristic or by
another learned component.

\subsubsection{Ninth meeting (30 April 2026)}

The ninth meeting confirmed that the partitioned one-shot strategy
worked. A greedy partitioner was already in place, not yet driven by a
GNN, but by hand-crafted priorities such as \textit{ASAP}, \textit{ALAP} and edge count —
whose objective was to minimise the number of edges cut at the
partition boundary, and thus the amount of synthetic memory traffic
needed to keep memory operations outside of the CGRA's ALU array. The
splitting was working and each subgraph was being mapped one-shot with
the SAT-MapIt-inspired one-shot mapper to produce reference labels for
this abstraction. From here the plan was
clean: use the original large graph, augmented with the per-partition
timesteps as an extra feature, to train a GNN that predicts the
schedule order (\textit{label0}); then feed \textit{label0} into the
partitioner to obtain better partitions, and finally validate each
partition with the simplified SAT mapper. This is the flow that ended up being reported in this
document.


\section{Development of the solution}
\glsadd{dfg}\glsadd{cgra}\glsadd{pe}\glsadd{dag}\glsadd{sat}\glsadd{smt}\glsadd{z3}\glsadd{alu}\glsadd{oneshot}\glsadd{mapping}\glsadd{mapper}\glsadd{lisagnn}\glsadd{gnn}\glsadd{json}\glsadd{label0}\glsadd{ai}

\subsection{Requirements}

The project was always very open-ended in terms of the technical solution, as long as it
iterated on state of the art approaches using \textit{AI}. One technical constraint was to
agree on a JSON format for the output since another project for the codegen part was being
developed in parallel, and the mapping output had to be clean and stable for that project to consume it without further negotiation.

The prototype does not cover cycle-by-cycle temporal scheduling. The SAT
\textit{mapper} only verifies the spatial validity of each partition, that is,
it assumes that internal dependencies are resolved by direct connections
between PEs in the same configuration.

\subsection{Architecture and technologies}

The solution was implemented in Python and organised as a modular pipeline:

\begin{center}
	\texttt{DFG generation} $\rightarrow$
	\texttt{LISA-GNN inference} $\rightarrow$
	\texttt{partitioning} $\rightarrow$
	\texttt{materialisation} $\rightarrow$
	\texttt{SAT mapping}
\end{center}

The generator creates synthetic data flow graphs. The LISA-GNN inference
module computes predictions for the graph nodes. The partitioner receives the
DFG, the CGRA description and, optionally, those predictions. The result is an
ordered sequence of partitions, each one converted into a materialised DFG.
Finally, each partition is verified by a SAT mapper implemented with
Z3\cite{z3} and exported as a PE configuration.

\subsubsection{DFG model}

A DFG is modelled as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) in which the nodes
represent operations and the edges represent data dependencies. Constants are
root nodes and memory operations are represented explicitly through
\texttt{load} and \texttt{store}. In the current model, all edges have zero
distance, that is, a dependency inside a partition must be satisfied in the
same one-shot configuration.

\subsubsection{CGRA model}

The CGRA is described by a grid of PEs. Each PE can support a subset of
operations and, in some architectures, only some PEs can perform memory
operations. The topology defines which PEs are directly connected. In the
current experiments a homogeneous $4 \times 4$ CGRA was used. Figure~\ref{fig:cgra-4x4} shows the canonical layout we
assume: a 2D grid of PEs, with data and instruction memories on the
side, where each PE is essentially an \textit{ALU} with a local register file
and can operate on the results delivered by its neighbouring PEs.

		data and instruction memories; each PE can operate on the results of

\subsubsection{One-shot mapping validity}

For a partition to be considered mappable, the SAT solver must find an
assignment of nodes to PEs that satisfies the following conditions:

Let $N$ be the set of instruction nodes of the partition, $E$ the set
of instruction-to-instruction edges, and $P$ the set of PEs of the
CGRA. For every $(n,e) \in N \times P$ we introduce a Boolean
placement variable
\[
	p_{n,e} \in \{0,1\}, \qquad p_{n,e} = 1 \iff \text{node } n
	\text{ is placed on PE } e.
\]
The mapping is then encoded by the following clauses:

\paragraph{(C1) Operation compatibility.}
Let $\mathit{op}(n)$ denote the opcode of node $n$ and $\mathit{ops}(e)$
the set of opcodes supported by PE $e$. For each node $n$ and each PE
$e$ that does not support $\mathit{op}(n)$, the placement is forbidden:
\begin{equation}
	\forall n \in N,\ \forall e \in P,\
	\mathit{op}(n) \notin \mathit{ops}(e) \implies \neg p_{n,e}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{(C2) Exactly one PE per instruction.}
Each instruction node is placed on exactly one PE, enforced as a
pseudo-Boolean equality:
\begin{equation}
	\forall n \in N,\ \sum_{e \in P} p_{n,e} = 1.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{(C3) At most one instruction per PE.}
Each PE may host at most one instruction of the partition:
\begin{equation}
	\forall e \in P,\ \sum_{n \in N} p_{n,e} \leq 1.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{(C4) Same-cycle nearest-neighbour dependencies.}
Let $\mathit{nbr}(a,b)$ denote that PEs $a$ and $b$ are directly
connected in the CGRA topology (or that $a = b$ when the model allows
intra-PE forwarding). For every internal dependency
$u \rightarrow v \in E$, at least one feasible neighbouring assignment
must be selected:
\begin{equation}
	\forall (u,v) \in E,\
	\bigvee_{\substack{a,b \in P \\ \mathit{nbr}(a,b)}}
	\bigl( p_{u,a} \wedge p_{v,b} \bigr).
\end{equation}
This is a simplification of SAT-MapIt's temporal forward-edge
constraint: dependent instructions may share the same partition (and
therefore the same one-shot cycle) as long as their PEs are physically
adjacent.

\paragraph{(C5) Memory-capable PEs.}
For each memory node $n$ (\texttt{load} or \texttt{store}) and each PE
$e$ that does not provide memory access ($e \notin \mathit{mem}$):
\begin{equation}
	\forall n \in N_{\mathit{mem}},\ \forall e \in P,\
	e \notin \mathit{mem} \implies \neg p_{n,e}.
\end{equation}
In the current experiments we assume a homogeneous CGRA in which every
PE supports every operation and every PE has memory access, so
$\mathit{mem} = P$ and (C5) is always satisfied;

\paragraph{Post-solution resource checks.}
Fan-in, fan-out and physical-link capacity are not encoded in the SAT
formulation. Once Z3 returns a model, the prototype reads back the
placements and rejects any solution in which, for the chosen limits, a
PE exceeds its outgoing or incoming edge budget, or a physical link
between two PEs is used by more edges than its capacity. A violation
here is reported back to the caller as an infeasible mapping rather
than as a SAT failure.

A structure such as $A \rightarrow C \leftarrow B$ is valid within a single
partition if $A$, $B$ and $C$ are placed on compatible PEs and if the PEs of
$A$ and $B$ can deliver their results directly to the PE of $C$, within the
limits of $C$'s inputs and the physical links. It should be noted that the
prototype covers spatial feasibility inside the partition, not temporal
scheduling.

\subsubsection{DFG partitioning}

When the input finite DFG does not fit in a single CGRA configuration, the graph is
split into a sequence of partitions $P_1, \ldots, P_K$. To preserve the order
of the dependencies, the partitioner enforces:

\begin{equation}
	partition(src) \leq partition(dest)
\end{equation}

for each edge $src \rightarrow dest$. This constraint prevents dependencies
that would have to flow back to a previous partition.

Two strategies were implemented:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{sat-min-k}: probes increasing numbers of partitions,
	      starting from a lower resource bound and validating each candidate
	      via SAT. When the search is exhaustive, this proves the smallest
	      feasible $K$ under the implemented model;
	\item \textbf{greedy}: builds a sequence of partitions through a greedy
	      pass over the ready nodes, optionally using priorities provided by
	      the LISA-GNN.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Materialisation of inter-partition dependencies}

An edge that crosses a partition boundary cannot be represented as a direct
connection between PEs, because the two operations belong to distinct
configurations. For a non-load producer, the cut edge $u \rightarrow v$ is
materialised as:

\begin{center}
	\texttt{P\_i: u -> synthetic\_store}
	\hspace{1cm}
	\texttt{P\_j: synthetic\_load -> v}
\end{center}

The two synthetic nodes share the same logical memory reference. When the
producer is an original \texttt{load}, the destination partition reloads the
same original memory reference and the source-side synthetic store is omitted.
The overall
approach is therefore a \textbf{partitioned one-shot mapping}: temporal
decomposition between partitions, spatial \textit{mapping} within each
partition.

\subsubsection{LISA-GNN integration}

The LISA-GNN integration follows the motivation of LISA\cite{lisa2022}:
leveraging learned information about the DFG structure to guide the
\textit{mapping}. In the prototype, the GNN predicts a per-node priority
\textit{label0}, which defines the exploration order of the ready nodes in
the greedy partitioner. The GNN does not replace the solver: the validity of
the \textit{mapping} still depends on the structural constraints and the SAT
verification.

\subsection{Developed solution}

The developed solution is a Python pipeline that takes a DFG and a CGRA
description as input and produces, for each one-shot partition of the
graph, a SAT-validated placement of the partition's instructions onto
the PEs of the CGRA. The pipeline is organised in five stages, each
implemented as an independent module and exercised by the same set of
end-to-end tests:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{DFG generation.} Either a synthetic graph is produced
	      by the in-tree generator (\texttt{dfg\_gen}), or an existing
	      JSON DFG is loaded from disk. The format follows the model
	      described in Section~3.2.1 — instruction nodes, constant nodes
	      and \texttt{load}/\texttt{store} memory nodes, with zero-distance
	      data dependency edges.
	\item \textbf{LISA-GNN inference (optional).} When enabled, the
	      LISA-GNN module reads the DFG and produces a per-node
	      \textit{label0} prediction, used downstream as a priority by
	      the greedy partitioner.
	\item \textbf{Partitioning.} The DFG is split into an ordered
	      sequence $P_1, \ldots, P_K$ of subgraphs that respect the
	      topological order of the original graph. Two strategies are
	      available: \texttt{sat-min-k}, which probes increasing values
	      of $K$ and validates each candidate via SAT, proving the smallest
	      feasible $K$ only when the search is exhaustive, and the
	      \textit{greedy} variant, optionally driven by the LISA-GNN
	      priorities.
	\item \textbf{Materialisation.} Cross-partition dependencies are
	      rewritten as synthetic memory operations: non-load producers use
	      paired synthetic \texttt{store}/\texttt{load} nodes, while cuts
	      from original loads are represented as reloads in the destination
	      partition. This yields one self-contained DFG per partition.
	\item \textbf{SAT mapping.} Each materialised partition is handed to
	      the SAT mapper described in Section~3.2.3, which either
	      returns a valid placement or rejects the partition.
\end{enumerate}

The resulting artefacts are a sequence of per-partition JSON mappings,
each one listing the PE assignment of every node together with the
operand wiring (PE-to-PE links, immediate constants and memory
references). This is the input consumed downstream by the code
generation work developed in parallel by the team colleague.

Two end-to-end examples are bundled with the prototype as smoke tests
and reference inputs: a synthetic matrix multiplication DFG and a
synthetic convolution DFG. Both fit in two one-shot partitions on a
homogeneous $4 \times 4$ CGRA and exercise the full pipeline from DFG
generation through SAT mapping. They are also the inputs used in the
validation reported in the next subsection.

example, the original DFG, the partitioning produced by the pipeline,
